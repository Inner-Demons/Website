How trauma can affect brain development in children and teens:

By Daniel

Problem statement:

Many students are deeply struggling with mental health due to trauma at a young age and are affected long after the event. Students don't know how harmful the effects of their trauma are nor where to go for help due to a lack of mental health services.

Need statement:

Teenagers who suffer from poor mental health need to be informed on how they could be affected and where they can get help in order to take action and minimize the effects of what's happened to them.

Evidence:

  • “The impact of child traumatic stress can last well beyond childhood. In fact, research has shown that child trauma survivors may experience:Learning problems, including lower grades and more suspensions and expulsions. Increased use of health and mental health services. Increase involvement with the child welfare and juvenile justice systems. Long-term health problems (e.g., diabetes and heart disease)” Trauma can cause a multitude of issues that affects teens in their daily life that many people don't even consider. This includes difficulties in their schooling. Despite this, places like schools offer very little in terms of mental health support which leaves students (especially low income students) not knowing where to go.
  • “More than two thirds of children reported at least 1 traumatic event by age 16” It's not like mental health in teens is uncommon either with many having suffered traumatic events before they are even 17. Even if the traumatic event does not cause long term issues it's absurd that mental health for teens is so understaffed when the issue is this widespread.
  • “Potentially traumatic events include: 1. Psychological, physical, or sexual abuse 2.Community or school violence 3.Witnessing or experiencing domestic violence 4.National disasters or terrorism 5.Commercial sexual exploitation 6.Sudden or violent loss of a loved one 7.Refugee or war experiences 8.Military family-related stressors (e.g., deployment, parental loss or injury) 9.Physical or sexual assault 10.Neglect 11.Serious accidents or life-threatening illness” Trauma can come from many places and it's important to know that what a teen might consider a normal event could be one that impacts their mental health. Many teens largely assume that many things in their past are not traumatic events and don't realize how their trauma is able to affect them as a result.

Source:

  • SAMHSA article

Imposter Syndrome:

By Tomas

Problem statement:

Imposter Syndrome causes extreme stress, anxiety, and burnout, typically in work or highly academic settings, in students or professors.

Need statement:

Students need a mentor in school to build a connection with and be able to talk to in order to feel less stressed and feel like they have truly earned their place.

Evidence:

  • According to an article by Crystal Raypole for healthline which was medically reviewed by Vara Saripalli, people who suffer from imposter syndrome are likely to write of success as luck, and believe that they did not earn their place and are putting up a front to their peers and professors.
  • According to the same article, a good way to deal with imposter syndrome is to acknowledge your feelings and share them, which can help them feel less overwhelming
  • Furthermore, building connections will help validate your own strengths and you will not have to do everything by yourself
  • Building connections will also help you realize you don't need to compare yourself with others, because everybody has their own strengths and talents, and nobody can do it all

Sources:

  • APA Article
  • NYT Article
